# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Dashboard Vaccine serves Mayo District public health operations across 14 service units.

Primary users are district leadership, the district vaccine team, district administrators, service-unit staff, and future field workers. District leadership needs a quick aggregate view without login friction. The district vaccine team and administrators need readiness, data-quality, and follow-up visibility. Service-unit staff need a clear path into operational workflows after staff login.

## Product Purpose

The product tracks child vaccine follow-up progress and service-unit reporting readiness for Mayo District.

The first public dashboard release provides one non-login aggregate executive view while keeping child-level, worker-level, row-level issue, import, approval, confirmation, export, and audit workflows behind role-controlled staff access.

Success means district leaders can see the correct high-level status quickly, service units understand whether reporting is complete, and no public surface exposes child-identifying or operational detail.

## Brand Personality

Official, warm, and accountable.

The interface should feel like a practical public health operations tool: calm, credible, Thai-first, and suitable for repeated use by district and service-unit staff. It should not feel like a marketing site or a generic SaaS analytics template.

## Anti-references

Avoid generic SaaS dashboards, purple or blue gradient hero styling, decorative charts, oversized marketing cards, fake medical imagery, dark dashboards without a clear usability need, and any UI that implies child-level or staff-level detail is public.

Avoid showing fake vaccine-progress numbers before the monthly reporting backend is ready. In the first release, the vaccine progress tab remains first but displays a transparent not-ready state when real monthly aggregate data is unavailable.

## Design Principles

1. Aggregate first, operational detail behind login.
2. Show incompleteness honestly instead of smoothing over missing or unready data.
3. Keep the first screen credible for district leadership and approachable for service units.
4. Prefer compact, readable dashboard density over decorative presentation.
5. Make privacy boundaries visible in the product shape, not only in backend checks.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Use Thai-first labels, high contrast, readable type sizes, and mobile layouts that avoid cramped tables or overlapping text. The public dashboard should remain usable for older staff and service-unit users on ordinary mobile devices.

Motion should be minimal and state-driven. Do not rely on color alone to communicate status; pair status colors with labels and counts.
